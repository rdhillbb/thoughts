# Thoughts from the Road to San Jose

*A response to Mani Thiru's LinkedIn post on Women, Climate Science, Inequality, Pollution, LGBTQ, Discrimination, Black, and Social Justice.*

---

After reading your post and during my two-hour drive to the AI conference in San Jose, my mind scribbled through several disjointed thoughts.

## The Flaw in Blind Justice

The law is supposed to be blind, and some argue that it is. However, there is a major flaw in this thinking: the implementation of the law is influenced by personal bias. If those enforcing the law carry biases, then the law itself is not truly blind. That is the real problem.

My father was once arrested because a woman of non-color claimed he yelled at her. He revealed this to me years later when he was unable to co-sign a small business loan. I am grateful he wasn't in Mississippi when it happened. I am sure my father did not, as that is not his character. So, when was it a crime to yell at someone? I am sure there is no such law on the books.

## Cognitive Dissonance and Critical Thinking

When a person is raised from birth to believe that a particular group is inherently "X," cognitive dissonance and confirmation bias take root for life. If that person lacks critical thinking skills or the intellectual capacity to understand, they cannot change — even with a two-by-four swung at them. You have a better chance of telling a tree to run to the store. The sad story is that a large percentage of folks fall into this category.

If you recognize that a certain percentage of the population has cognitive dissonance and poor critical thinking, and that they can be easily influenced, you can rally them. By doing so, you can manipulate them into acting against their own interests, distracting them while filling your pockets with their money.

## The American Lens

Think about it. *Make America Great Again.* America is flawed. The U.S. standing in the world only declined until the 45th and 47th president took office and stated it (false flag). Depending on what ethnic group you belong to, from an internal perspective — "What do you mean, *Again*?"

I live outside the U.S., and from my perspective, most Americans are largely unaware of the rest of the world. Their perceptions are shaped by certain news media that use vocabulary at a fourth-grade level, making it easy to lead them like the Pied Piper.

## An Expatriate's Reflection

America has changed since I left in 1989. I enjoy diversity — meeting people from other parts of the world, other religions, and other cultures. I try to listen and learn and put my American ways in my back pocket.
